# Security Controls

Security controls are safeguards designed to reduce cybersecurity risks and protect an organization's assets. They help prevent, detect, or respond to security incidents.

Security controls can be categorized into three types:

## Administrative Controls

Administrative controls are policies, procedures, and management practices that guide employee behavior.

Examples include:

- Security awareness training
- Password policies
- Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)
- Risk assessments
- Security audits

These controls primarily reduce human-related risks.

---

## Technical Controls

Technical controls are implemented using hardware or software.

Examples include:

- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Firewalls
- Antivirus software
- Encryption
- Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
- Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)

These controls help prevent unauthorized access and detect malicious activity.

---

## Physical Controls

Physical controls protect facilities and hardware from unauthorized physical access.

Examples include:

- Security guards
- CCTV
- Door locks
- Badge access systems
- Biometric authentication

---

# Security Frameworks

Security frameworks provide organizations with structured guidance for implementing and improving cybersecurity programs.

Rather than prescribing specific technologies, frameworks define best practices, governance, and risk management processes.

Well-known frameworks include:

- NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)
- NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)
- ISO/IEC 27001
- CIS Controls

---

# Compliance

Compliance means following laws, regulations, standards, and organizational policies.

Organizations implement compliance programs to:

- Reduce legal risk
- Protect customer information
- Meet industry requirements
- Improve trust with customers and stakeholders

Failure to comply may result in:

- Financial penalties
- Legal consequences
- Loss of reputation
- Operational disruption

---

# CIA Triad

The CIA Triad forms the foundation of information security.

## Confidentiality

Confidentiality ensures that information is accessible only to authorized individuals.

Examples include:

- Encryption
- Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
- Multi-Factor Authentication
- Least Privilege

---

## Integrity

Integrity ensures that information remains accurate, complete, and trustworthy.

Examples include:

- Hashing
- Digital signatures
- Version control
- File integrity monitoring

---

## Availability

Availability ensures systems and information remain accessible whenever authorized users need them.

Examples include:

- Backups
- Disaster recovery plans
- High availability systems
- Redundant infrastructure

---

# Threat Actors

Threat actors are individuals or groups that intentionally exploit vulnerabilities.

Common threat actors include:

- Cybercriminals
- Nation-state attackers
- Hacktivists
- Insider threats

One of the most dangerous insider threats is a disgruntled employee because they already possess legitimate access to organizational resources.

---

# Security Ethics

Cybersecurity professionals must always act ethically.

Important ethical principles include:

- Protect confidentiality
- Respect privacy
- Follow applicable laws
- Remain unbiased
- Report evidence accurately
- Follow organizational policies

Even when requested by supervisors, security professionals should never violate security policies or bypass established procedures.

---

# Key Lessons Learned

This module emphasized that cybersecurity is more than implementing technical controls.

Organizations require a combination of:

- Security controls
- Security frameworks
- Compliance requirements
- Ethical decision-making

Together, these elements reduce organizational risk and strengthen overall security posture.
