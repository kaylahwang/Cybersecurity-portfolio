# Cybersecurity Glossary

## Module 3 – Protect Against Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities

This glossary summarizes the key cybersecurity terms introduced in Module 3. These concepts form the foundation of security controls, risk management, compliance, and ethical decision-making.

---

# Administrative Controls

Policies, procedures, and management practices designed to reduce security risks by guiding employee behavior.

**Examples**

* Security awareness training
* Password policies
* Risk assessments
* Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

---

# Availability

One of the three principles of the CIA Triad. Availability ensures that systems, applications, and data remain accessible to authorized users whenever needed.

**Examples**

* Backups
* Disaster recovery
* High availability
* Redundant systems

---

# CIA Triad

A foundational information security model consisting of:

* **Confidentiality** – Prevent unauthorized access to information.
* **Integrity** – Ensure data remains accurate and unaltered.
* **Availability** – Ensure systems and information remain accessible.

---

# CIS Controls

A prioritized set of cybersecurity best practices developed by the Center for Internet Security (CIS) to improve organizational security and reduce cyber risk.

---

# Compliance

The process of adhering to laws, regulations, standards, and organizational policies that govern cybersecurity and information protection.

---

# Confidentiality

The principle of ensuring that sensitive information is accessible only to authorized users.

**Common Controls**

* Encryption
* Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
* Access Control
* Least Privilege

---

# Cybersecurity Framework

A structured set of standards, guidelines, and best practices that helps organizations manage cybersecurity risks.

**Examples**

* NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)
* NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)
* ISO/IEC 27001
* CIS Controls

---

# Encryption

The process of converting readable information (plaintext) into unreadable ciphertext to protect it from unauthorized access.

---

# GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

A European Union regulation that protects personal data and privacy.

Organizations must implement appropriate safeguards and report certain personal data breaches within **72 hours**.

---

# HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)

A U.S. law that protects Protected Health Information (PHI).

Key rules include:

* Privacy Rule
* Security Rule
* Breach Notification Rule

---

# Information Security

The practice of protecting information and information systems from unauthorized access, disclosure, modification, destruction, or disruption.

Its primary objective is to preserve the CIA Triad.

---

# Integrity

The principle of ensuring that information remains accurate, complete, and trustworthy throughout its lifecycle.

**Examples**

* Hashing
* Digital signatures
* File integrity monitoring

---

# ISO/IEC 27001

An international standard for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an Information Security Management System (ISMS).

---

# Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

An authentication method that requires two or more verification factors before granting access.

**Examples**

* Password
* Authentication app
* Hardware token
* Fingerprint

---

# NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

A voluntary framework developed by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) to help organizations identify, manage, and reduce cybersecurity risk.

**Core Functions**

* Identify
* Protect
* Detect
* Respond
* Recover

---

# NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)

A framework that provides a structured process for identifying, assessing, implementing, and continuously monitoring security controls throughout a system's lifecycle.

---

# Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

Information that can identify an individual.

**Examples**

* Full name
* Email address
* Phone number
* Home address
* Date of birth

---

# PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)

An international security standard that helps organizations protect payment card information and reduce payment fraud.

---

# Physical Controls

Security measures that protect facilities, equipment, and physical assets from unauthorized access.

**Examples**

* CCTV
* Security guards
* Locks
* Badge access
* Biometric authentication

---

# Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP)

A security principle stating that users should receive only the minimum level of access required to perform their job responsibilities.

---

# Protected Health Information (PHI)

Health-related information protected under HIPAA.

**Examples**

* Medical records
* Diagnoses
* Treatment history
* Insurance information

---

# Risk

The potential for loss or damage when a threat exploits a vulnerability.

Risk is commonly evaluated based on:

* Likelihood
* Impact

---

# Security Control

A safeguard or countermeasure implemented to reduce cybersecurity risk and protect organizational assets.

**Types**

* Administrative Controls
* Technical Controls
* Physical Controls

---

# Security Ethics

The professional responsibility to protect information, respect privacy, follow laws and organizational policies, and make objective, evidence-based security decisions.

---

# SOC Report

An independent assessment that evaluates an organization's internal controls.

**Common Types**

* SOC 1
* SOC 2

SOC 2 evaluates controls related to:

* Security
* Availability
* Processing Integrity
* Confidentiality
* Privacy

---

# Technical Controls

Security mechanisms implemented through hardware or software.

**Examples**

* Firewalls
* Antivirus
* Encryption
* MFA
* Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)
* Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)

---

# Threat

Any event or circumstance that has the potential to exploit a vulnerability and negatively affect an organization's assets.

**Examples**

* Malware
* Phishing
* Insider threats
* Ransomware

---

# Threat Actor

An individual or group that intentionally attempts to compromise systems or data.

**Common Threat Actors**

* Cybercriminals
* Nation-state actors
* Hacktivists
* Insider threats

---

# Vulnerability

A weakness in a system, application, process, or security control that could be exploited by a threat actor.

**Examples**

* Weak passwords
* Misconfigured systems
* Unpatched software
* Outdated applications

---

# Key Takeaway

Understanding these foundational terms is essential for building a strong cybersecurity knowledge base. These concepts support effective risk management, security governance, compliance, and ethical decision-making, and they serve as the foundation for more advanced cybersecurity topics covered throughout the Google Cybersecurity Professional Certificate.
